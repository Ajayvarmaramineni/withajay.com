There are nights when the stars are out but offer no guidance. You look up and see all that light — ancient, indifferent — and it doesn't help. Not because the sky isn't beautiful, but because you're not sure what question you're even asking.

That was one of those nights.

I sat outside longer than I should have. The air had that particular April coolness — not cold enough to drive you in, just cold enough to keep you aware of your own skin. I had a cup of tea that went cold. I forgot about it completely.

I kept turning over the same thoughts, the same worries, the same half-finished plans. It's a particular kind of exhaustion — not the kind that comes from doing too much, but the kind that comes from not knowing what to do at all.

> The stars were out, but they offered no guidance. It was one of those nights where the world felt too wide to navigate.

I think what unsettles me most isn't being lost. It's the feeling that I should know by now. That there's a certain age or stage at which the direction becomes clear, and I'm watching that deadline approach without the clarity arriving.

But then I thought about every person I admire who has described feeling exactly this. Not as a low point they escaped from quickly, but as a place they sat with for a long time. A season they passed through slowly, learning its geography.

Maybe being lost isn't a failure of navigation. Maybe it's the navigation itself.

I didn't resolve anything that night. The tea stayed cold. The stars stayed indifferent. But somewhere toward midnight, I stopped fighting the feeling and just let myself be in it — confused, uncertain, still here.

That felt like enough.
