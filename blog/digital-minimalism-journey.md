Last year I deleted Instagram off my phone for thirty days. Not the account — just the app. And for the first week, I kept reaching for it reflexively, several times a day, opening the spot where it used to be before remembering it wasn't there anymore.

That reflex was the data point I needed.

## What I was actually doing

Here's what I realized I was using Instagram for: about fifteen percent genuine connection — seeing what people I care about are doing, sharing moments I wanted to share. The other eighty-five percent was something harder to name. Filling small silences. Avoiding the mild discomfort of just existing without stimulation for thirty seconds.

That's the honest accounting. The platform wasn't the problem. The habit was.

## The attention economy

There's a phrase I keep coming back to: **your attention is the product**. Every feature in every app you use was designed, tested, and optimized to keep you using it longer. The infinite scroll, the variable reward of notification, the red badges — all of it is engineered.

None of this is hidden or conspiratorial. It's just business. But it means that using these tools requires actual intentionality, because the tools themselves are actively working against restraint.

> Your attention is the product. Using these tools requires intentionality, because they are actively working against restraint.

## What I changed (and kept)

After the thirty days, I didn't swear off social media. That felt like overcorrection. Instead, I made a few structural changes:

- **No apps on the phone.** I use Instagram and Twitter through the browser, which adds just enough friction to break the reflex loop.
- **Specific times.** Once in the morning, once in the evening. Not throughout the day.
- **Notifications off.** For almost everything. I check things; they don't interrupt me.
- **Weekly review.** A quick honest look at whether my digital habits are serving me or the reverse.

None of these are dramatic. They're small frictions, deliberately introduced to create space between impulse and action.

## The unexpected gains

What I didn't anticipate: how much mental quiet accumulated when I wasn't filling every pause with content.

The idle moments — waiting in line, walking between places, sitting with the end of a coffee — started to have different texture. I noticed things. Had thoughts that went somewhere. Felt a kind of boredom that wasn't unpleasant.

Boredom, it turns out, is where a lot of good thinking happens. We've almost completely eliminated it, and I don't think that's worked out well for us.

## Still a work in progress

I'm not going to claim I've solved this. I still lose half an hour to YouTube occasionally. I still reach for my phone when I'm uncomfortable. The habits are sticky.

But the gap between impulse and action has gotten wider. That gap is where choice lives. And more of my digital time now feels chosen rather than defaulted into.

That's the goal — not perfection, just more intentional navigation of tools that are very good at navigating you.
