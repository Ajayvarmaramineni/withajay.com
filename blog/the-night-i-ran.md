I didn't plan to go out that night. It was past ten, the kind of hour when you've already mentally closed the day and changed into something comfortable. But something in me was restless in a way I couldn't reason with, so I put on my shoes and went.

The streets were mostly empty. That particular quality of silence that comes after the evening crowd has gone home but before the late-night ones have emerged. A few windows lit up. A cat somewhere. The hum of whatever city always hums.

I started walking. Then, without deciding to, I started running.

I'm not a runner, not really. I don't have the stride or the discipline or the carefully calibrated playlist. I just started moving faster and my feet found a rhythm and for a few minutes, everything that had been loud in my head went quiet.

> Learning to move slower so life doesn't rush past me.

That's the irony that hit me halfway through — I was running to find stillness. Moving fast to feel calm. The body doing what the mind couldn't: burning off the excess, making room.

I ran for maybe twenty minutes. Probably less. I ended up at a small park a few blocks from home, slightly out of breath, hands on my knees, looking at the orange glow the streetlights made on the pavement.

I thought about pace. Not running pace — life pace. How fast I've been trying to move. How much I've been trying to fit in. There's ambition and then there's the kind of speed that makes you miss things. I've been close to the second one lately.

The walk home was slower. Deliberately slower. I noticed the cracks in the sidewalk, the smell of someone's late dinner through an open window, the particular shade of purple the sky goes just before it goes fully dark.

It turns out I didn't need to run. I needed to slow down enough to notice I was moving at all.
