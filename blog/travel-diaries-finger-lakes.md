There's a particular quality to stillness that's hard to find in a city. Not silence exactly—birds, wind, the occasional distant motor—but a kind of quietness that moves at the pace of weather rather than traffic. The Finger Lakes have that.

We arrived on a Saturday morning and the lake was flat in the way that lakes are on calm days, reflecting the sky almost perfectly. It made the world look doubled. I stood at the edge for a long time doing nothing.

The hikes were short but honest. None of them made me feel like an athlete; all of them made me feel like a person paying attention. There's a difference.

> The best hikes aren't the hardest ones. They're the ones where you actually notice what you walked through.

I took a lot of photographs. Not the sweeping landscape kind—those rarely come out the way you want them to—but close ones. The way the light hit the water at a particular angle in the late afternoon. A single wildflower at the edge of a path. The look on someone's face when they spotted a hawk overhead.

The camera becomes a reason to slow down. To look twice. To be where you are.

We ate at a winery and felt very slightly pretentious and then got over it because the view was too good to waste on self-consciousness. The wine was good. The company was better. The lake, as always, was exactly what we needed.
