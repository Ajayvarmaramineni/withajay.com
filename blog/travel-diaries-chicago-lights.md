It had rained all morning, and by the time we arrived downtown, the streets were still wet and the city was doing that thing where every surface becomes a mirror. The skyline reflected in the sidewalk. The streetlights blurred into long vertical streaks on the roads. Chicago in the rain is a different city—softer at the edges, every surface briefly collaborative.

We walked the Riverwalk first because it seemed right to start at water level and work upward. The architecture was relentless and deliberately so—every building competing, asserting, making an argument. I like cities that argue with you.

> Chicago doesn't ask you to be comfortable. It asks you to be present. I think those are different requests.

Millennium Park was less crowded than I expected, maybe because of the earlier rain. Cloud Gate—"The Bean"—does exactly what public art should do: it makes you stop. You see yourself in it, distorted and multiplied, and for a moment the city bends around you.

The pizza is a whole event. I ordered deep dish because you have to, even knowing you'll leave slightly regretful and completely satisfied. It arrived like architecture. Layers. Construction. Ambition. The pizza slice that stole the show was not photogenic, which I think is part of the appeal.

We walked until the streetlights came on and the wet streets went amber, and then we walked a little more.

Chicago gave us a full day of itself. That felt like enough, and somehow still not enough.
