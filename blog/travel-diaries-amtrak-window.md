There is something about train travel that slows your thinking down to the pace of the landscape.

I had a window seat, which felt lucky in the way small things feel lucky when you've been paying attention. The car was half full, quiet in the particular way of places where strangers have agreed, without speaking, to give each other space. Someone two rows back was reading. A child near the front was watching the window the way children do—like the world outside is a film made specifically for them.

We passed through towns I'll never visit. I watched them from the train the way you watch something through glass—present but not touching, aware but not involved. A crossing. A field. A parking lot lit up in the gray afternoon. A water tower with a name I couldn't read before we were past it.

> The window frame turns the ordinary into composition. Everything becomes worth looking at when it's moving past.

I wrote things down in the notes app on my phone, not for any particular reason, just because the motion and the looking were producing thoughts I didn't want to lose. Whether any of them made sense when I read them later, I'm honestly not sure. That feels like the right outcome.

There's a version of travel that's about arrival. This was the other kind—the kind that's about the between. The miles and the light and the way the afternoon changes color while you're sitting still and the world is moving.

I got where I was going. But the journey was what I needed.
