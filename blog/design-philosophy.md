I think about design a lot — not just as a professional discipline, but as a way of thinking about nearly everything. How things are arranged, what they communicate, what gets emphasized and what gets quietly removed.

Good design, at its best, is almost invisible. You interact with something and it just *works*, and you don't stop to notice how or why. That invisibility is the craft. It took enormous effort to produce something that looks effortless.

## Form follows function, but function is a choice

There's the old principle: *form follows function*. Design the container around what the container needs to do. Don't decorate; solve.

I believe in this, mostly. But I've started to think that "function" is doing a lot of unexamined work in that sentence. Function for whom? In what context? Toward what end?

A website that maximizes time-on-site is highly functional — by one metric. But if that time is spent in low-quality engagement, chasing dopamine hits that don't deliver, then the function served isn't actually serving the user.

Design is always in service of something. The question worth asking is: what?

## Constraints as creative tools

Some of the best design I've encountered came from severe constraints. Limited color palettes. Single-column layouts. Plain text with no images. Black on white.

When you can't rely on decoration, you have to rely on clarity. The writing has to be good. The structure has to do work. Every element earns its place because there's no room for elements that don't.

> When you can't rely on decoration, you have to rely on clarity. Constraints make the work honest.

This has changed how I approach things outside of design too. What happens when you remove the scaffolding — the signaling, the performance, the accumulated complexity — and work with just what remains? Usually, what remains is more interesting.

## The aesthetics of restraint

There's a particular aesthetic I'm drawn to: the kind that looks like nothing has been designed at all, but where every choice was deliberate. The Japanese concept of *ma* — the beauty of negative space, of what's left out.

It's not minimalism for its own sake. It's the recognition that restraint is a form of respect. For the viewer's attention. For the content itself. For the idea that not everything needs to be amplified.

In a culture that defaults to MORE — more features, more content, more stimulation — choosing to do less, and doing it very well, is quietly countercultural.

## What I try to apply

When I'm building something — a presentation, a page, an argument — I try to ask a few questions:

- **What is this actually for?** Not what could it be for, what is it actually for?
- **What can I remove?** Every element should earn its place.
- **Is this honest?** Does the form reflect the content, or is it masking something?
- **Would it work in black and white?** Color and styling can compensate for weak structure. If the bones aren't good, decoration won't fix that.

These questions don't always produce elegant answers. Sometimes the honest answer is that something is complicated, and the design needs to reflect that complexity rather than smooth over it.

But more often, they produce something simpler. And simpler, done well, tends to last.
