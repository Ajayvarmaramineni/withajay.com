We took the early bus. The kind that leaves before the city wakes up, when the streets are still dark and the only people at the station are travelers and workers and the particular type of person who simply enjoys being up before everyone else.

Boston arrived the way old cities do—slowly, then all at once. The highway gave way to neighborhoods, and the neighborhoods gave way to cobblestone, and suddenly we were somewhere that felt genuinely old in a way that American cities rarely do.

The weekend had no agenda, which turned out to be the agenda.

> There is a particular freedom in walking a city without a map, in letting the next street decide where you're going.

We walked the Freedom Trail without finishing it, which felt appropriate somehow—you shouldn't have to complete a thing to learn something from it. We found a coffee shop down a side street that had no seating and one person behind the counter who knew everyone's name but ours. We had the coffee standing up, on the sidewalk, watching people go past.

The museum you shouldn't skip is the Isabella Stewart Gardner Museum, and I'll say no more than that, because the best way to experience it is to walk in knowing nothing. Just go.

Lunch was clam chowder in a bread bowl. It felt mandatory and it delivered.

Boston doesn't try to impress you. It's just there—steady and layered and a little worn in all the right places. I liked it immediately.
