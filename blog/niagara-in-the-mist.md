Nobody warns you about the sound. You see the pictures—the wide sweep of water, the arc of the falls, the mist that softens everything into a kind of dream—but the pictures are silent. In person, Niagara hits you with noise first, a low continuous roar that you feel before you fully hear it, like standing next to a subwoofer at a concert except the subwoofer is the size of a river.

We got there on a Friday morning, early enough that the tourist crush hadn't fully arrived. The air was already damp. We were given blue plastic ponchos that rustled with every step, and we looked, collectively, like a group of very enthusiastic garbage bags headed toward something beautiful.

The Maid of the Mist boat takes you close enough that "mist" feels like a polite understatement. It's rain. Full lateral rain, except the rain is coming from the falls themselves, and the falls are louder than anything I've been close to outdoors. I stood at the railing, gripping it, face absolutely soaked, and grinned like I was seven years old.

> There's something about being that small next to something that massive that rearranges your priorities without asking permission.

I thought about that during the boat ride back. How rarely we let ourselves be genuinely dwarfed by something. We're so careful about appearing in control, appearing unbothered, appearing like we've seen it all before. But I had not seen this before, and I was glad I let it show on my face.

We had lunch afterward at a diner nearby—clam chowder, a grilled cheese that arrived in triangles, hot coffee. The kind of meal that tastes twice as good because you're still warm from having been cold. We talked about where else we wanted to go, what we wanted to see, the particular way light hit the water at a certain angle right before the falls.

Travel is never just the landmark. It's the poncho you almost lost to the wind. It's the grin you can't wipe off for the rest of the day.
